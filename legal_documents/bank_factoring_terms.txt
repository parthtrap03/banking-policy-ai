BANK FACTORING SERVICES – TERMS AND CONDITIONS (INDIA)
Compiled from Major Indian Banks' Factoring Products
Applicable Banks: SBI, HDFC Bank, ICICI Bank, Axis Bank, Kotak Mahindra Bank, IndusInd Bank, Yes Bank, IDFC First Bank, RBL Bank, Bank of Baroda, Punjab National Bank, Canara Bank, Union Bank, Indian Bank

## 1. ELIGIBILITY CRITERIA

### 1.1 Seller (Assignor) Eligibility
(a) The seller must be a company, partnership firm, proprietorship, LLP, or MSME registered in India.
(b) The seller must have a minimum operational history of 2 years (1 year for MSMEs on TReDS).
(c) The seller must have a minimum annual turnover of Rs. 50 lakh (varies by bank; some banks accept Rs. 25 lakh for MSMEs).
(d) The seller must not be listed on RBI's defaulter list or CIBIL/CRILC negative list.
(e) The seller must have audited financial statements for the past 2 financial years.

### 1.2 Buyer (Debtor) Eligibility
(a) The buyer must be a company, PSU, or government entity with a valid credit rating.
(b) The buyer must have a minimum credit rating of BBB (or equivalent) from a SEBI-registered credit rating agency.
(c) The buyer must not be classified as a willful defaulter or fraud by any bank.
(d) The buyer must accept the invoices on the factoring platform within the prescribed time (typically 7-15 business days).

### 1.3 Invoice Eligibility
(a) Minimum invoice value: Rs. 25,000 (or as specified by the bank).
(b) Maximum invoice tenure: 180 days from the date of invoice (some banks allow up to 120 days only).
(c) The invoice must represent a genuine trade transaction for supply of goods or services.
(d) The invoice must not be disputed, overdue, or subject to any lien or encumbrance.
(e) The invoice must not have been previously assigned to another factor.

## 2. ACCOUNT OPENING AND DOCUMENTATION

### 2.1 Required Documents (Seller)
(a) KYC documents (PAN card, Aadhaar, passport, or voter ID of all partners/directors).
(b) Proof of business registration (Certificate of Incorporation, GST registration, Udyam registration for MSMEs).
(c) Audited financial statements for the last 2 financial years.
(d) Bank statements for the last 12 months.
(e) Board resolution authorising factoring (for companies).
(f) List of buyers with expected monthly invoice volumes.
(g) Sample invoices and purchase orders.

### 2.2 Required Documents (Buyer)
(a) KYC documents of authorised signatories.
(b) Board resolution authorising participation in the factoring programme.
(c) Credit rating certificate from a SEBI-registered agency.
(d) Undertaking to pay the factor directly upon notice of assignment.

### 2.3 Agreement Execution
(a) Factoring Agreement: Master agreement between the seller and the factor covering all future assignments.
(b) Deed of Assignment: Executed for each batch of invoices assigned.
(c) Irrevocable Payment Instruction: Buyer's undertaking to pay the factor.
(d) Security documents (if applicable): Pledge of inventory, assignment of insurance, personal guarantee of promoters.

## 4. PAYMENT TERMS AND ADVANCE RATES

### 4.1 Advance Rate
(a) Banks typically advance 80% to 90% of the invoice value at the time of assignment.
(b) The remaining 10% to 20% (margin/retention) is released after the buyer pays the full invoice amount.
(c) For non-recourse factoring, the advance rate may be lower (70% to 85%) due to higher credit risk.

### 4.2 Payment Collection
(a) Upon assignment, the buyer is notified to make payment directly to the factor's designated account.
(b) If the buyer pays the seller instead of the factor (post-notice), the seller must remit the payment to the factor within 2 business days.
(c) Partial payments by the buyer are applied first towards the factor's charges, then towards the principal.

### 4.3 Settlement Timeline
(a) T+0 to T+1: Assignment and advance disbursement to seller.
(b) T+30 to T+180: Buyer pays the factor on the due date.
(c) T+0 to T+3 after buyer payment: Margin amount released to seller.
(d) If buyer defaults: Recourse exercised against seller (in recourse factoring) within 7 business days.

## 5. DEFAULT AND REMEDIES

### 5.1 Events of Default
(a) Buyer fails to pay within 90 days of the due date (NPA classification triggers).
(b) Seller assigns the same invoice to multiple factors.
(c) Seller provides fraudulent or misleading information about the receivable.
(d) Insolvency or winding-up proceedings initiated against the seller or buyer.
(e) Material adverse change in the financial condition of the seller or buyer.

### 5.2 Remedies Available to the Factor
(a) Recourse Factoring: Demand full repayment from the seller within 7 days of the default event.
(b) Non-Recourse Factoring: Invoke credit insurance (if any) or absorb the loss.
(c) File a claim with the Debt Recovery Tribunal (DRT) for amounts above Rs. 20 lakh.
(d) Invoke SARFAESI Act provisions (if security interest is registered).
(e) Refer the matter to arbitration or the MSME Facilitation Council.
(f) Report the default to CIBIL/CRILC.

### 5.3 Penal Charges on Default
(a) Interest on overdue amounts: Base discount rate + 2% to 4% per annum.
(b) Legal and recovery costs: Borne by the defaulting party.
(c) Blacklisting: The defaulting seller/buyer may be blacklisted from the factoring platform and all participating banks.

## 6. TERMINATION

### 6.1 Termination by the Seller
(a) The seller may terminate the factoring arrangement with 30 days' written notice.
(b) All outstanding assigned receivables must be settled before termination.
(c) Any unadjusted margin amounts will be refunded within 15 business days.

### 6.2 Termination by the Factor
(a) The factor may terminate the arrangement immediately upon:
    - Breach of any term of the factoring agreement.
    - Material adverse change in the seller's financial condition.
    - Regulatory directive requiring termination.
(b) The factor may suspend new assignments with 7 days' notice without terminating existing ones.

### 6.3 Consequences of Termination
(a) All assigned receivables remain with the factor until maturity/collection.
(b) The seller's obligation to repay (in recourse factoring) survives termination.
(c) Confidentiality and indemnity obligations survive for 3 years post-termination.

## 7. CONFIDENTIALITY AND DATA PROTECTION

### 7.1 Confidentiality Obligations
(a) Both parties must keep all transaction details confidential.
(b) Disclosure is permitted only:
    - To regulatory authorities (RBI, SEBI, FIU-IND).
    - Under court order or legal process.
    - To credit rating agencies (with consent of the other party).

### 7.2 Data Protection (DPDP Act 2023 Applicability)
(a) The factor is a "Data Fiduciary" under the DPDP Act with respect to personal data of the seller's promoters and authorised signatories.
(b) Consent must be obtained before processing personal data for KYC and credit assessment.
(c) Data retention: Personal data shall be retained for a maximum of 5 years after account closure (as required by RBI KYC norms).
(d) Data breach notification: The factor must notify the Data Protection Board within 72 hours of discovering a breach.

## 8. GOVERNING LAW AND JURISDICTION

### 8.1 Governing Law
All factoring arrangements shall be governed by:
(a) Factoring Regulation Act, 2011 (as amended 2021).
(b) Reserve Bank of India Act, 1934.
(c) Indian Contract Act, 1872.
(d) Sale of Goods Act, 1930.
(e) SARFAESI Act, 2002 (for security interest enforcement).
(f) Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023 (for data-related obligations).
(g) Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996 (for dispute resolution).

### 8.2 Jurisdiction
(a) Courts at the location of the factor's registered office shall have exclusive jurisdiction.
(b) For TReDS transactions, disputes may be referred to the platform's dispute resolution mechanism before approaching courts.
(c) MSME-related disputes shall first be referred to the MSME Facilitation Council under the MSME Development Act, 2006.

## 9. SPECIAL CONDITIONS FOR MSMEs

### 9.1 MSME Payment Protection
(a) Under MSME Development Act 2006, buyers must pay MSME suppliers within 45 days of acceptance of goods/services.
(b) Delayed payment attracts interest at 3x the bank rate notified by RBI.
(c) MSMEs can file complaints with the MSME Facilitation Council for recovery.

### 9.2 TReDS Benefits for MSMEs
(a) Government mandate: All CPSEs (Central Public Sector Enterprises) with turnover above Rs. 500 crore must onboard TReDS.
(b) GST-registered MSMEs get priority for TReDS registration.
(c) Interest rates on TReDS are typically 2-3% lower than traditional bank factoring because financing is based on the buyer's credit rating.

### 9.3 MSME Factoring Limits
(a) Single invoice limit: Up to Rs. 2 crore on TReDS.
(b) Aggregate limit per MSME: Based on the buyer's approved credit limit (no statutory cap for MSMEs).
(c) No collateral required for TReDS-based factoring (unsecured).
